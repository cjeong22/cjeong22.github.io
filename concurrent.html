Concurrent Master's Program

Introduction

So Brown has this cool program called the concurrent master's program where undergraduates can obtain both master's and bachelor's degrees in their four years at Brown. The details of this program are pretty obfuscated so I'm writing this to help clear up the process for any interested undergraduates.

Motivations

Many of my peers choose to pursue this degree option as a means to boost their chances in the H1B lottery. Many more hope to qualify themselves for ML Engineer and Data Scientist roles. I personally decided to pursue a concurrent master's degree because it's financially smart and I wanted to preemptively check the box for more senior roles that may require an advanced degree. I also probably would've taken all of these courses anyways even without the option to pursue a concurrent master's degree, so it's convenient to get another degree.

Details

Obviously in order to obtain both a bachelor's and master's degree, one must complete the course requirements for both a bachelor's and a master's degree. For computer science, this entails completing a bachelor of science in cs or one of the joint concentrations (math-cs, apma-cs, cs-econ) and completing a master of science in cs. Furthermore, there can be a two course overlap between the bachelor's and master's degrees. Where the requirements get confusing is in the breadth and depth requirements Brown imposes: those pursuing a concurrent degree must complete a total of ten courses in the physical sciences, social sciences, life sciences, and humanities, with a minimum of two courses in each section. Within the area of study that one wants to obtain their master's degree in, the breadth requirements used must not count for concentration credit. For example, I'm pursuing a bachelor's degree in cs, which falls under physical sciences; therefore, my breadth requirements that I use for physical sciences cannot also be used to complete my cs degree. Furthermore, Brown stipulates that the breadth courses must fall under departments that reflect those of a "liberal arts curriculum." For example, the only departments whose courses are accepted for physical sciences are math, chemistry, physics, and geology. I have seen people get around this "liberal arts" requirement, but they were required to take additional breadth courses. Overall, the whole process is very confusing and I recommend talking with the concurrent master's dean.
